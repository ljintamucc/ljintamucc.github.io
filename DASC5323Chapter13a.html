Chapter 13 Correspondence Analysis¶

Introduction¶

Correspondence Analysis (CA) is an exploratory multivariate technique used to analyze and visualize relationships in categorical data, typically organized in a contingency table (e.g., rows = groups, columns = categories, entries = counts).

It plays a role similar to Principal Component Analysis (PCA) but for frequency / count data rather than continuous variables.
The goal is to represent both rows and columns as points in a low-dimensional space (usually 2D), where distances reflect patterns of association:

  • Row points that are close together have similar response profiles.
  • Column points that are close together tend to be chosen/observed with similar rows.
  • Proximity between a row point and a column point indicates strong association.

CA is widely used in marketing, survey analysis, social sciences, and ecology.

Basic Setup and Notation¶

Suppose we have an $I \times J$ contingency table of counts: $$ F = [f_{ij}], \quad i = 1,\dots,I,\; j = 1,\dots,J, $$ with total $$ N = \sum_{i=1}^I \sum_{j=1}^J f_{ij}. $$

Define the relative frequency matrix: $$ P = \frac{1}{N} F = [p_{ij}]. $$

Row and column marginal proportions: $$ r_i = \sum_j p_{ij}, \quad c_j = \sum_i p_{ij}, $$ and let $D_r$ and $D_c$ be diagonal matrices with $r_i$ and $c_j$ on the diagonals.

If rows and columns were independent, wed expect $$ p_{ij}^{(\text{indep})} = r_i c_j. $$

CA studies the deviations from independence: $$ P - r c^T. $$

These deviations are standardized and decomposed using singular value decomposition (SVD): $$ S = D_r^{-1/2}(P - r c^T)D_c^{-1/2}, $$ $$ S = U \Lambda V^T. $$

The low-dimensional principal coordinates (to plot) are:

  • Row points: $$ F_r = D_r^{-1/2} U \Lambda, $$
  • Column points: $$ F_c = D_c^{-1/2} V \Lambda. $$

Plotting the first one or two dimensions from these coordinates gives the correspondence map.

The distances between points indicate how similar or different their preference profiles are. Points that lie close together share similar response patterns, suggesting similar movie taste distributions across age groups or genres, while points that are far apart indicate more distinct preferences. The axes Dimension 1 (horizontal) and Dimension 2 (vertical) summarize the main sources of variation in the data. Dimension 1 captures the strongest contrast between agegenre associations, while Dimension 2 explains a secondary and independent pattern of variation in viewing preferences.

Older viewers (4160) appear near Drama, and seniors (61+) are closest to Romance, indicating that older audiences prefer more story-driven and emotionally engaging genres. The horizontal dimension (Dimension 1) separates younger vs. older age groups, illustrating a generational divide in movie preferences, whereas the vertical dimension (Dimension 2) differentiates between Action- and Drama-oriented preferences, capturing differences between energetic and emotional storytelling styles.

🌐 Three-Way (Multiple) Correspondence Analysis (MCA)¶

When a dataset contains more than two categorical variables, a simple two-way Correspondence Analysis (CA) is no longer sufficient because it only handles one contingency table (rows columns). To explore relationships among three or more categorical variables, we use Multiple Correspondence Analysis (MCA), which generalizes CA to handle multiway tables or categorical data frames.

Typical Scenarios:

  • The HairEyeColor dataset (Hair Eye Sex)
  • Surveys with multiple categorical questions (e.g., Education Income Preference Gender)
  • Market research (Brand Product Age Group Region)

MCA visualizes how the categories of all variables relate to each other and to the overall structure of the dataset.

Mathematical Idea (Simplified)

For a two-way table, CA decomposes: $$ S = D_r^{-1/2}(P - r c^T) D_c^{-1/2}. $$

For a three-way table, we first reshape it into a binary indicator matrix, where each row corresponds to an observation, and each column represents a category (1 = present, 0 = absent).

MCA then performs a singular value decomposition (SVD) on this indicator matrix to find latent dimensions that best represent the associations among all category levels.

Example Dataset: Hair Eye Sex

Rs built-in HairEyeColor dataset provides counts of 592 students classified by three categorical variables:

  • Hair color: Black, Brown, Red, Blond
  • Eye color: Brown, Blue, Hazel, Green
  • Sex: Male, Female

We can analyze this as a three-way contingency table or convert it to a two-dimensional data frame suitable for MCA.
