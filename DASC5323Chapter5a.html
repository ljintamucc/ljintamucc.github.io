Chapter 5 MANOVA¶

Hotellings T Test and Multivariate Analysis of Variance (MANOVA)
Hotellings T test is the multivariate counterpart of the two-sample t-test, while MANOVA extends ANOVA to assess group differences across multiple dependent variables simultaneously.

Review of hypothesis testing¶

After collecting sample data through an observational study or experiment, statistical inference enables analysts to evaluate evidence supporting a claim about the population from which the sample was taken. Hypothesis testing, also referred to as tests of significance, are the methods to either accept (fail to reject) or reject claims based on sample data. The objective of hypothesis testing is to access the amount of the evidence in the sample in favor of a particular claim regarding population parameter(s).

The p-value, or probability value, is a measure in hypothesis testing to determine the amount of the evidence against the null hypothesis.

$$ P-value = P( \mbox{data or more exetreme situations} | H0) $$

It is the probability of obtaining results as extreme as, or more extreme than, the observed results, under the assumption that the null hypothesis is true. It tells you how likely it is to observe the data, or more extreme data, if the null hypothesis is actually true. A smaller p-value indicates stronger evidence against the null hypothesis, suggesting that the observed data is unlikely to have occurred if the null hypothesis were true. Typically, if the p-value is less than a predetermined significance level ($\alpha$), the null hypothesis is rejected, indicating that there is sufficient evidence to support the alternative hypothesis. Conversely, if the p-value is greater than the significance level, fail to reject the null hypothesis , suggesting that there is not enough evidence to support the alternative hypothesis.

One sample t-test:¶

The one-sample t-test is a statistical procedure used to determine whether the population mean of a single sample differs significantly from a hypothesized value. It is typically applied when you have a single group of observations and want to compare their mean value on a continuous variable against a hypothesized benchmark.

Example:
Odometers are used to gauge automobile mileage, but how accurately do they reflect the actual distance traveled? Suppose 12 cars travel a predetermined distance of exactly 10 miles, and the recorded mileage values from their odometers are:

$9.8,\; 10.1,\; 10.3,\; 10.2,\; 9.9,\; 10.4,\; 10.0,\; 9.9,\; 10.3,\; 10.0,\; 10.1,\; 10.2$

(a) Test whether the odometer is inaccurate in terms of the population mean¶

  • Hypotheses:
    $H_0: \mu = 10$
    $H_1: \mu \ne 10$

  • Method: One-sample t-test (assuming data are approximately normally distributed).
    Normality can be checked using a QQ plot.

  • Results:
    $t = 1.8638$ (calculated by formula or R)
    $p$-value $= 0.08924$

  • Conclusion: Since $p$-value $> 0.05$, we fail to reject $H_0$. There is insufficient evidence to conclude that the population mean of the odometer readings systematically deviates from 10.

(b) Test whether the odometer systematically overestimates the actual mileage¶

  • Hypotheses:
    $H_0: \mu = 10$
    $H_1: \mu > 10$

  • Results:
    $t = 1.8638$ (calculated by formula or R)
    $p$-value $= 0.04462$

  • Conclusion: Since $p$-value $< 0.05$, we reject $H_0$. There is sufficient evidence to conclude that the population mean of the odometer readings is greater than 10.

The one-sample Hotelling’s T2 test¶

The one-sample Hotellings T test is the multivariate counterpart of the traditional one-sample or paired Students t-test. While the one-sample t-test compares the population mean of a single variable to a specified value, Hotellings one-sample T test extends this framework to cases involving two or more response variables (though it also reduces to the t-test when there is only one response variable).

The primary purpose of the one-sample Hotellings T test is to determine whether the population mean vector is equal to a given mean vector when there are multiple response variables.

Formally, the hypotheses can be stated as:

$H_0: {\mu} $ = a given vector of known numbers

$H_1: {\mu} \ne $ the given vector of known numbers

Formula for one-sample Hotelling's T^2 test¶

$$ T^2 = (\bar{x} - \mu_0)^{\prime} \left(\tfrac{1}{n}\mathbf{S}\right)^{-1} (\bar{x} - \mu_0) = n(\bar{x} - \mu_0)^{\prime}\mathbf{S}^{-1}(\bar{x} - \mu_0) $$$$ T^2 \sim \frac{(n-1)p}{\,n-p\,} F_{p,\,n-p} $$$$ P\!\left(F_{p,\,n-p} > \frac{(n-p)}{(n-1)p}T^2 \right) $$

Review of Two-Sample t Test¶

Researchers conducted a study to compare the efficacy of two mosquito repellents. Two groups of participants were tested, and the outcome was measured as the number of mosquito bites experienced:

  • Repellent A: 5, 12, 33, 17, 9, 3, 6, 12, 20, 14, 7, 12, 25, 6, 4, 5
  • Repellent B: 12, 19, 36, 15, 18, 7, 5, 12, 26, 57, 10, 11, 22, 13, 11, 5

At a significance level of 0.01, the study aims to determine whether there is sufficient evidence to support the claim that Repellent A is more effective than Repellent B.

To compare the two groups in terms of their population means, we set up the hypotheses:

  • Null hypothesis:
    $$H_0: \mu_1 = \mu_2$$

  • Alternative hypothesis:
    $$H_1: \mu_1 < \mu_2$$

where $\mu_1$ is the mean number of bites after using Repellent A and $\mu_2$ is the mean number of bites after using Repellent B.

The two-sample Hotelling’s T2 test¶

The two-sample Hotellings T test is the multivariate extension of the two-sample t-test. It is used to determine whether the mean vectors of two groups differ significantly across multiple correlated dependent variables.

$H_0: (\mu_1, \mu_2, \cdots, \mu_k) = (v_1, v_2, \cdots, v_k)$

where $(\mu_1, \mu_2, \cdots, \mu_k)$ and $(v_1, v_2, \cdots, v_k)$ are the population mean vectors of two groups. The purpose of the two-sample Hotellings T2 test is to test if two population mean vectors of two groups are the same or not.

\begin{align} \mathbf{S}_p &= \frac{(n_1 - 1)\mathbf{S}_1 + (n_2 - 1)\mathbf{S}_2}{n_1 + n_2 - 2} \\[2mm] T^2 &= \frac{n_1 n_2}{n_1 + n_2} (\mathbf{\bar{X}}_1 - \mathbf{\bar{X}}_2)^\top \mathbf{S}_p^{-1} (\mathbf{\bar{X}}_1 - \mathbf{\bar{X}}_2) \\[1mm] F &= \frac{(n_1 + n_2 - p - 1) T^2}{(n_1 + n_2 - 2)p} \sim F_{p,\, n_1 + n_2 - p - 1} \end{align}$$ \begin{aligned} \mathbf{\bar{X}}_1 &\text{ = mean vector of Group 1} \\ \mathbf{\bar{X}}_2 &\text{ = mean vector of Group 2} \\ \mathbf{S}_1 &\text{ = covariance matrix of Group 1} \\ \mathbf{S}_2 &\text{ = covariance matrix of Group 2} \\ \mathbf{S}_p &\text{ = pooled covariance matrix} \\ n_1, n_2 &\text{ = sample sizes of the two groups} \\ p &\text{ = number of dependent variables} \end{aligned} $$

$p\text{-value} = P\!\left(F_{p,\, n_1 + n_2 - p - 1} > F\right)$

#Self defined Function for two sample Hotelling t test 
TwoSampleT2Test = function(X, Y)
{
nx = nrow(X)
ny = nrow(Y)
#The difference between two sample mean vectors
delta = colMeans(X) - colMeans(Y)
    
#This is the number of variables
p = ncol(X)
Sx = cov(X)
Sy = cov(Y)

#To obtain the pooled covaraince matrix of two groups
S_pooled = ((nx-1)*Sx + (ny-1)*Sy)/(nx+ny-2)

#Hotelling T
T_squared =  (nx*ny)/(nx+ny) * t(delta) %*% solve(S_pooled) %*% (delta)
    
#F-statistic
statistic = T_squared * (nx+ny-p-1)/(p*(nx+ny-2))
    
p_value = pf(statistic, p, nx+ny-p-1, lower.tail=FALSE)
return(list(Test.Statistic=statistic, p.value=p_value, df1=p, df2=nx+ny-p-1))
}

Review of ANOVA¶

Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) is a statistical technique used to compare the means of multiple independent populations. It is most commonly applied when there are three or more groups to determine whether at least one group mean is significantly different from the others.

When comparing the means of only two groups, a two-sample t-test is the appropriate method.
ANOVA can be viewed as a generalization of the t-test: while the t-test evaluates mean differences between two populations,
one-way ANOVA extends this approach to situations involving three or more populations.

ANOVA is to test the null hypothesis that all group means are equal:

$$ H_0: \mu_1 = \mu_2 = \cdots = \mu_k $$

against the alternative hypothesis that at least one mean differs:

$$ H_1: \text{Not all } \mu_i \text{ are equal.} $$

ANOVA partitions the total variation in the data into two components:

  1. Between-group variation differences due to group means.
  2. Within-group variation random variation within each group.

The ratio of these two sources of variation, summarized by the F-statistic, determines whether the observed group differences are statistically significant.

For the validity of ANOVA results, several assumptions need to be satisfied:

  1. Independence

    • The observations must be independent both within and across groups.
    • Typically ensured through proper random sampling or random assignment in experiments.

  2. Normality

    • The data within each group should be approximately normally distributed.
    • ANOVA is fairly robust to moderate violations of this assumption, especially when sample sizes are large and roughly equal across groups (Central Limit Theorem).

  3. Homogeneity of Variances (Equal Variances)

    • The population variances of all groups should be equal:
      $$ \sigma_1^2 = \sigma_2^2 = \cdots = \sigma_k^2 $$
    • This can be checked using tests such as Levenes test or Bartletts test.

Multivariate ANOVA (MANOVA)¶

A statistical method used to examine differences among multiple groups when there are two or more dependent variables considered simultaneously. MANOVA tests whether the mean vectors of the dependent variables differ significantly across groups, providing a multivariate extension of ANOVA.

Assumptions¶

  1. Multivariate Normality

    • The data of each group, or the residuals of all groups, should follow a multivariate normal distribution.
    • Can be assessed using a multivariate QQ plot of the residuals.

  2. Homogeneity of VarianceCovariance Matrices

    • The variancecovariance matrices should be equal across groups.
    • This can be tested using Boxs M test, which typically applies a more conservative alpha level (e.g., 0.01) when assessing significance.

The Boxs M test (often called Boxs Test of Equality of Covariance Matrices) is a multivariate test used to check an important assumption of equal variances.

MANOVA Example: Chemical Analysis of Romano-British Pottery¶

Researchers analyze Romano-British pottery to investigate differences in chemical composition across pottery types or production sites. Each pottery sample contains measurements of multiple chemical elements (e.g., aluminum, iron, magnesium), making this a multivariate dataset.

Pottery shards were collected from five sites in the British Isles:

  • L: Llanedyrn
  • C: Caldicot
  • I: Isle Thorns
  • A: Ashley Rails

Each sample was returned to the laboratory for chemical assay, where concentrations of several chemical elements were measured, including:

  • Al: Aluminum
  • Fe: Iron
  • (Additional chemicals can be listed here)

Objective:
MANOVA is used to determine whether the chemical composition of pottery depends on the site of origin.

Hypotheses:

  • Null hypothesis (H): The chemical content of pottery is independent of the site. That is, the population mean vectors of the measured chemicals are the same across all sites.
  • Alternative hypothesis (H): The chemical content of pottery depends on the site.

MANOVA Calculation and Assumptions:
MANOVA requires several assumptions, including multivariate normality and homogeneity of variancecovariance matrices.

There are four widely used test statistics for MANOVA:

  1. Wilks Lambda
  2. Roys Largest Root
  3. Pillais Trace
  4. Hotellings Trace

In R, Pillais Trace is commonly used. It is converted to an F-statistic to test the significance of group mean differences. While other tests (Wilks Lambda, Roys Largest Root, Hotelling-Lawley) can also be used, Pillais Trace is considered the most robust and powerful, especially when assumptions are slightly violated.

Multivariate Regression¶

Multiple regression models are used to establish the relationship between a dependent variable (i.e. an outcome of interest) and more than 1 independent variable. Multivariate regression can be used to establish the relationship between more than 1 dependent variable and some independent variable(s).

The number of depedent variables.

Multivariate linear model: $$ \begin{align} \mathbf{Y}=\mathbf{B}^T\mathbf{X}+\boldsymbol{\varepsilon} \end{align} $$

where $\mathbf{Y}=(Y_1,\ldots,Y_q)^T$, $\mathbf{X}=(X_1,\ldots,X_p)^T$ , $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}:=(\varepsilon_1,\ldots,\varepsilon_q)^T$ and $$ \begin{align*} \mathbf{B}=\begin{pmatrix} \beta_{11} & \ldots & \beta_{1q} \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \beta_{p1} & \ldots & \beta_{pq} \end{pmatrix}_{p\times q}. \end{align*} $$

$$ \begin{align} \begin{pmatrix} Y_{11} & \ldots & Y_{1q} \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ Y_{n1} & \ldots & Y_{nq} \end{pmatrix}_{n\times q}\!\!\! &= \begin{pmatrix} X_{11} & \ldots & X_{1p} \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ X_{n1} & \ldots & X_{np} \end{pmatrix}_{n\times p} \begin{pmatrix} \beta_{11} & \ldots & \beta_{1q} \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \beta_{p1} & \ldots & \beta_{pq} \end{pmatrix}_{p\times q}\!\!\! + \begin{pmatrix} \varepsilon_{11} & \ldots & \varepsilon_{1q} \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \varepsilon_{n1} & \ldots & \varepsilon_{nq} \end{pmatrix}_{n\times q}, \end{align} $$

When $q = 1$, and $p>1$ it is a multiple regression.

The underlying assumptions:¶

  1. Linearity
    Each predictor should have a linear relationship with each outcome. Nonlinear relationships can bias the results.

  2. Independence
    Observations should be independent. If they are not (e.g., in time-series or clustered data), standard errors may become unreliable.

  3. Multivariate Normality
    Error terms are assumed to follow a multivariate normal distribution.

    • Even if this assumption is violated, coefficient estimates remain unbiased.
    • However, confidence intervals and hypothesis tests may lose accuracy.
    • While tests like ShapiroWilk can assess normality for individual outcomes, multivariate normality is best evaluated with methods such as Mardias test, which accounts for correlations among residuals.

  4. Homoscedasticity
    The variance of errors should be constant across predictors and outcomes. If not, the model becomes less efficient.

  5. No Multicollinearity issues
    Predictors should not be perfectly correlated, as this prevents the model from estimating their separate effects.
