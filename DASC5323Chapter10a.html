Chapter 10 Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA)¶

Usually, the correlation is to quantify the linear association bewtween two quantiative variables. Canonical correlation analysis (CCA) is to identify and quantify the associations within two sets of multivariate quantitative variables. It is appropriate in the same situations where multivariate regression would be. It determines orthogonal linear combinations of the variables (a set of canonical variates) within each set that best explain the variability both within and between sets. CCA allows us to summarize the relationships into fewer statistics while preserving the main facets of the relationships. In this way, the motivation for CCA is very similar to PCA as another dimension-reduction technique.

Canonical Correlation is an extension of the correlation of two univariate variables (x, y) to the relationship between two multivariate variables (X, Y).

Applications:

Psychological (3 variables) and Academic Variables (4 variables): A researcher has data on three psychological variables, four academic variables. The researcher seeks to indetify how the psychological variables correlate with academic performance, by the canonical variables to capture the association between the two sets of variables.

How strong the Psychological variables and Academic Variables are related?

Objectives:

  1. Conducting CCA using R or a suitable tool.

  2. Determining the optimal number of canonical variate pairs to consider.

  3. Interpreting the scores of canonical variates.

  4. Interpret the relationships between variables across the two sets.

The objective of CCA is to find vectors $a$ and $b$ such that the correlation between $Xa$ and $Yb$ is maximized. $$Maximize: cor(Xa, Yb)$$

Subject to: $$ var(Xa) = 1, var(Yb) = 1 $$

More specifically: it finds linear combinations of the data, (a set of linear combinations) named U and V. U corresponds to the linear combinations from the first set of variables, X, and V corresponds to the second set of variables, Y. Each member of U is paired with a member of V.

\begin{align} U_1 & = a_{11}X_1 + a_{12}X_2 + \dots + a_{1p}X_p \\ U_2 & = a_{21}X_1 + a_{22}X_2 + \dots + a_{2p}X_p \\ & \vdots \\ U_p & = a_{p1}X_1 +a_{p2}X_2 + \dots +a_{pp}X_p\\ & \\ V_1 & = b_{11}Y_1 + b_{12}Y_2 + \dots + b_{1q}Y_q \\ V_2 & = b_{21}Y_1 + b_{22}Y_2 + \dots +b_{2q}Y_q \\ & \vdots \\ V_p & = b_{p1}Y_1 +b_{p2}Y_2 + \dots +b_{pq}Y_q\end{align}

First canonical variate pair: $\left( U _ { 1 } , V _ { 1 } \right)$ with the coefficients $a_{11}, a_{12}, \dots, a_{1p}$ and $b_{11}, b_{12}, \dots, b_{1q}$ being selected to maximize the canonical correlation of the first canonical variate pair, under the constraint that variances of the two canonical variates in that pair are equal to one $$\text{var}(U_1) = \text{var}(V_1) = 1$$.

#"Dimension 1" and "Dimension 2" are the first two canonical axes, which explain most of the relationship between environmental variables and species composition.
#Red dots usually represent environmental variables (constraints). Blue triangles represent species scores or canonical coefficients of response variables.Points closer to each other (species and environmental variables) have stronger associations.
#The direction from the origin (0,0) indicates the gradient of variation explained by that environmental variable. For example, species near a specific red dot are more strongly influenced by that environmental variable.
