A random variable is a value determined by chance (informal definition).
We usually use a capital letter to denote a random variable.

Example:
Let $X$ be the result of rolling a fair (balanced) die once.

Then $X$ may take the values $1, 2, 3, 4, 5,$ or $6$ by chance.

How Do We Describe a Random Variable Mathematically?¶

A probability distribution tells us the possible values of a random variable and the corresponding probabilities of those values.

For the die example, $$ P(X = k) = \frac{1}{6}, \quad k = 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6. $$

Types of Random Variables¶

Discrete Random Variables¶

The possible values can be listed or counted.
The probability distribution of a discrete random variable is called a Probability Mass Function (PMF).

$$ P(x) = P(X = x) = p_x, $$

where $x$ is one of a list of specific values.

Continuous Random Variables¶

Any value within a given range can be a possible value.

Example:
The speed of a train any value between 0 and 100 miles per hour.

Mean (Expected Value)¶

The mean, or expected value, of a discrete random variable $X$ is $$ E[X] = \sum_x x p_x, $$ where $p_x$ is the probability that $X$ takes the value $x$.

The expected value is a weighted average of all possible values.

Example¶

Suppose an individual plays a gambling game with the following outcomes and probabilities:

Outcome ($) -2.00 0.00 20.00 100.00
Probability 0.75 0.15 0.049 0.001

Find the expected value of the distribution.

$$ \begin{aligned} E[X] &= (-2)(0.75) + (0)(0.15) + (20)(0.049) + (100)(0.001) \\ &= -1.5 + 0 + 0.98 + 0.10 \\ &= -0.42. \end{aligned} $$

This means that, in the long run, the average loss per game is $0.42.

Variance of a Discrete Random Variable¶

The variance of a discrete random variable is given by $$ \operatorname{Var}(X) = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2. $$

2.1.1 Special Discrete Random Variables¶

A. Bernoulli Distribution¶

A random variable $X$ is said to have a Bernoulli distribution with parameter $p$ if $$ X \sim \mathrm{Bernoulli}(p), \quad 0 \le p \le 1. $$

  • Support: $x \in \{0, 1\}$
  • Probability Mass Function (PMF):
$$ P(X = x) = p^x (1 - p)^{1 - x}, \quad x \in \{0, 1\}. $$

Example:
Toss a coin once, where the probability of obtaining a head (success) is $p$.

B. Binomial Distribution¶

A random variable $X$ is said to have a Binomial distribution with parameters $n$ and $p$ if $$ X \sim \mathrm{Binomial}(n, p), \quad n \in \mathbb{N}, \; 0 \le p \le 1. $$

  • Support: $x = 0, 1, \dots, n$
  • Probability Mass Function (PMF):
$$ P(X = x) = \binom{n}{x} p^x (1 - p)^{n - x}, \quad x = 0, 1, \dots, n. $$

A binomial random variable models the number of successes in a fixed number of independent and identical trials, where each trial has the same probability of success.

Example:

Let $X$ be the number of heads obtained when tossing a fair coin 9 times.

Then $X$ has a binomial distribution with

  • $n = 9$ (the number of trials), and
  • $p = 0.5$ (the probability of success in each trial).

C. Poisson Distribution¶

A random variable $X$ is said to have a Poisson distribution with parameter $\lambda$ if $$ X \sim \mathrm{Poisson}(\lambda), \quad \lambda > 0. $$

  • Support: $x = 0, 1, 2, \dots$
  • Probability Mass Function (PMF):
$$ P(X = x) = e^{-\lambda} \frac{\lambda^x}{x!}, \quad x = 0, 1, 2, \dots $$

A Poisson random variable models the number of occurrences of an event in a fixed amount of time, space, or distance, assuming that the event occurs at a constant average rate $\lambda$ and that occurrences are independent.

Examples:

  1. The number of phone calls received in one hour ($0, 1, 2, \dots$).
  2. The number of hurricanes in Texas next year.
  3. The number of DNA mutations in a segment of DNA.

Parameter $\lambda$: the rate of occurences.

Choosing the correct discrete distribution depends on what is fixed and what is being counted.¶

Use the Poisson distribution when:

  • An average rate (mean) of events per unit (time, page, distance, area, etc.) is given, and
  • You are asked to find the probability of observing $n$ events in a fixed interval.

Typical wording includes phrases such as on average, rate per unit, or expected number of events.

Example:

  • The number of typos per 100 pages
  • The number of phone calls in two hours
  • The number of accidents per mile

Use the Binomial distribution when:

  • There is a fixed number of trials $n$, and
  • Each trial can be grouped into only two outcomes (success/failure), with
  • A known (or implied) probability of success $p$, and
  • You are asked for the probability of $k$ successes out of $n$ trials.

Typical wording includes phrases such as out of, exactly $k$, or fixed number of trials.

Example:

  • Number of defective items in a sample of $n$ products
  • Number of heads in tossing a coin $n$ times

Use the Negative Binomial distribution when:

  • The probability of success $p$ is fixed, and
  • Trials continue until (precede, before) a specified number of successes $r$ is reached, and
  • You are asked about the number of failures before the $r$th success (or equivalently, the total number of trials until $r$ successes).

Typical wording includes phrases such as until, before, or how many trials are needed.

Example:

  • Number of customers who do not buy before the $5$th sale
  • Number of non-defective items inspected before finding $r$ defective ones

2.2 Continuous distributions¶

To model the driving speed of a randomly selected car in the highway, we may use a density curve: in mathematics, we call it "probablity density function " (pdf)

Let $X$ be a continuous random variable with probability density function $f(x)$.

Probability¶

$$ P(a \le X \le b) = \int_a^b f(x)\,dx $$

Expected Value (the mean)¶

$$ \mathbb{E}[X] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x f(x)\,dx $$

Variance¶

$$ \mathrm{Var}(X) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} (x - \mathbb{E}[X])^2 f(x)\,dx $$

or $$ \mathrm{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - (\mathbb{E}[X])^2 $$

2.2.1 Normal distributions¶

History: The Normal distribution was first introduced by Abraham de Moivre in 1733 to approximate binomial probabilities. Later, Carl Friedrich Gauss (hence "Gaussian") popularized it in the early 19th century for analyzing astronomical and measurement errors. Its importance comes from the Central Limit Theorem, which states that sums of many independent random variables tend to follow a Normal distribution, regardless of the original distributions.

Normal (Gaussian) Distribution $N(\mu,\sigma^2)$ has a Probability Density Function as $$ f(x) = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}} \exp\left(-\dfrac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right) $$

where

  • $\mu$: mean (location / center of the distribution)
  • $\sigma$: standard deviation (spread)
  • $\sigma^2$: variance

A normal distribution is bell curved, symetrical, the mean is at $\mu$ and the variance is $\sigma^2$
