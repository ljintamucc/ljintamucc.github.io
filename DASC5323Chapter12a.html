Chapter 12 🧩 Independent Component Analysis (ICA)¶

Introduction¶

Independent Component Analysis (ICA) is a powerful statistical and computational technique for identifying hidden source signals from observed mixtures. It assumes that each observed variable is a linear combination of several statistically independent, non-Gaussian latent sources, and aims to recover those sources using only the observed mixtures. Unlike Principal Component Analysis (PCA), which decorrelates data by maximizing variance along orthogonal directions, ICA goes beyond decorrelation and seeks components that are as statistically independent as possible, not merely uncorrelated.

ICA uncovers independent latent causes underlying the data, making it ideal for separating distinct signal sources. For instance, it can isolate brain signal components in EEG recordings or separate individual instruments from a blended audio track. In contrast, Factor Analysis focuses on uncovering latent constructs that explain correlations among observed variables, for example, identifying underlying traits such as anxiety, sociability, or intelligence from psychological survey data.

Because of its ability to disentangle complex mixtures, ICA has become fundamental in modern applications across neuroscience, telecommunications, image processing, finance, and econometrics, where multiple independent processes contribute to the observed data.

Motivating Example: The Cocktail Party Problem¶

Imagine that you are in a room where two people are speaking simultaneously. You have two microphones, placed at different locations, recording the overlapping voices. Each microphone captures a mixture of both speakers sounds.
Let the microphone signals be $x_1(t)$ and $x_2(t)$, representing sound amplitudes at time $t$, and the true speech sources be $s_1(t)$ and $s_2(t)$. Then each observed signal is a weighted sum of the two true voices:

$$ \begin{cases} x_1(t) = a_{11}s_1(t) + a_{12}s_2(t), \\ x_2(t) = a_{21}s_1(t) + a_{22}s_2(t), \end{cases} $$

or equivalently,

$$ \mathbf{x}(t) = A \mathbf{s}(t), $$

where

  • $\mathbf{x}(t) = [x_1(t), x_2(t)]^T$ observed signals,
  • $\mathbf{s}(t) = [s_1(t), s_2(t)]^T$ unknown independent sources,
  • $A = [a_{ij}]$ mixing matrix that depends on geometry and acoustic paths.

If $A$ were known, the inverse problem would be trivial:

$$ \mathbf{s}(t) = A^{-1} \mathbf{x}(t). $$

However, in reality, both $A$ and $\mathbf{s}(t)$ are unknown only $\mathbf{x}(t)$ is observed.

The challenge is to recover the original speech signals using only the mixed recordings. This is known as the cocktail party problem, and ICA provides an elegant, general solution. By assuming that the sources $s_1(t)$ and $s_2(t)$ are statistically independent, ICA estimates both the mixing matrix $A$ and the independent sources simultaneously.

In practice, ICA recovers signals $\hat{s}_1(t)$ and $\hat{s}_2(t)$ that are nearly identical to the true speech sources, up to permutation and sign changes both of which are inconsequential.

Applications of ICA¶

(a) Brain Signal Separation — EEG Analysis¶

Consider electrical recordings of brain activity, such as electroencephalograms (EEGs).
EEG data consist of electrical potentials recorded at multiple scalp locations.
Each electrodes signal represents a mixture of multiple underlying sources of neural activity within the brain.

This situation is analogous to the cocktail party problem: we observe only mixtures of unknown neural processes.
Applying ICA to EEG data allows the extraction of independent components representing distinct physiological processes, such as:

  • Specific brain regions activity
  • Eye-blink artifacts
  • Muscle or cardiac noise

ICA has become a fundamental tool in neuroscience for isolating and interpreting meaningful signals from complex multichannel recordings.

(b) Feature Extraction and Data Representation¶

Another important use of ICA is in feature extraction. In digital signal or image processing, it is often desirable to find efficient representations of data for compression, denoising, or recognition. Traditional linear transforms such as the Fourier, Haar, or Cosine transforms are commonly used, each with its own mathematical advantages.

However, ICA provides a data-driven transformation: it estimates the linear transformation directly from the data, yielding basis functions adapted to the statistical structure of the dataset.

For example, when ICA is applied to patches of natural images, the resulting basis functions resemble localized edge detectors similar to receptive fields in the human visual cortex. Each image patch can be represented as a linear combination of these basis functions, with coefficients that are statistically independent.
This makes ICA a powerful method for automatic feature discovery in vision and signal analysis.

General ICA Model¶

In the general case of $n$ observed mixtures:

$$ \mathbf{x} = A \mathbf{s}, $$

where

  • $\mathbf{x} = (x_1, \ldots, x_n)^T$ are observed mixtures,
  • $\mathbf{s} = (s_1, \ldots, s_n)^T$ are independent, non-Gaussian sources,
  • $A$ is an invertible mixing matrix.

The ICA objective is to estimate a demixing matrix $W$ such that:

$$ \mathbf{s} = W \mathbf{x}, $$

and the recovered components $s_i$ are statistically independent.

Assumptions for Identifiability¶

  1. Statistical independence:
    $$ P(s_1, s_2, \ldots, s_n) = \prod_i P(s_i) $$
  2. Non-Gaussianity: At most one component can be Gaussian.
  3. Linear and instantaneous mixing: No time delays or convolutions.

Under these assumptions, ICA can uniquely recover the sources up to permutation and scaling.

Preprocessing: Centering and Whitening¶

Prior to ICA estimation, the observed data $\mathbf{x}$ are usually:

  • Centered:
    $$ \mathbf{x}_c = \mathbf{x} - E[\mathbf{x}] $$
  • Whitened:
    transform $\mathbf{x}_c$ so that
    $$ \mathrm{Cov}(\mathbf{x}_c) = I $$

Whitening (often via PCA) simplifies the ICA problem since it ensures the mixed data have uncorrelated, unit-variance dimensions making the effective mixing matrix orthogonal.

Estimation Principle: Maximizing Non-Gaussianity¶

The intuition behind ICA is rooted in the Central Limit Theorem:

Linear combinations of independent non-Gaussian variables tend to be more Gaussian than the original variables.

Therefore, to recover the independent components, ICA searches for directions $w$ such that $y = w^T x$ is maximally non-Gaussian.

Common measures of non-Gaussianity:¶

  • Kurtosis: $$ \text{Kurt}(y) = E[y^4] - 3(E[y^2])^2 $$
  • Negentropy: $$ J(y) = H(y_{\text{gauss}}) - H(y), $$ where $H(y)$ is the differential entropy, and $y_{\text{gauss}}$ is Gaussian with the same variance.

Maximizing these measures leads to statistically independent components.

FastICA Algorithm¶

The FastICA algorithm (Hyvrinen, 1999) is a fixed-point method for estimating $W$:

For whitened data $x$:

  1. Initialize a random unit vector $w$.
  2. Update iteratively: $$ w^{\text{new}} = E[x g(w^T x)] - E[g'(w^T x)] w, $$ where $g(u)$ is a nonlinear contrast function (e.g. $g(u) = \tanh(u)$).
  3. Normalize: $$ w^{\text{new}} \leftarrow \frac{w^{\text{new}}}{\|w^{\text{new}}\|} $$
  4. Decorrelate between components.
  5. Repeat until convergence.

## ICA Teaching Example in R
## Simulated Cocktail Party Data


# Load required package
library(ggplot2)

set.seed(121)
n <- 2000
t <- seq(0, 8 * pi, length.out = n)

s1 <- sin(t)                        # Smooth source (e.g., periodic)
s2 <- sign(rnorm(n)) * rexp(n, 1)   # Spiky, heavy-tailed source

S <- cbind(s1, s2)                  # True sources

# 2. Mixing matrix and observed mixtures
A <- matrix(c(0.6, 0.4,
              0.2, 0.8),
            nrow = 2, byrow = TRUE)
X <- S %*% t(A)                     # Mixed signals
colnames(X) <- c("x1", "x2")

# 3. Combine data for plotting
ica_data <- data.frame(
  time = t,
  s1 = S[, 1],
  s2 = S[, 2],
  x1 = X[, 1],
  x2 = X[, 2]
)


library(tidyverse)
ica_long <- ica_data %>%
  select(time, s1, s2, x1, x2) %>%
  pivot_longer(cols = -time, names_to = "Signal", values_to = "Value")

ggplot(ica_long, aes(x = time, y = Value, color = Signal)) +
  geom_line(linewidth = 0.6) +
  facet_wrap(~ Signal, ncol = 2, scales = "free_y") +
  theme_minimal(base_size = 12) +
  labs(title = "Simulated ICA Example: True Sources and Mixed Signals",
       x = "Time", y = "Amplitude") +
  theme(legend.position = "none")

In the R code, the two sources are generated as:

$$ \begin{aligned} s_1(t) &= \sin(t), \\ s_2(t) &= \text{sign}(z_t) \cdot \text{Exp}(1), \end{aligned} $$

where $z_t \sim N(0,1)$ and $\text{Exp}(1)$ denotes an exponential random variable.

Thus:

  • $s_1(t)$ is a smooth, periodic (Gaussian-like) source,
  • $s_2(t)$ is a non-Gaussian, heavy-tailed source with random spikes.

These two signals are statistically independent they do not share a common distribution or temporal dependence.

We define the mixing matrix $A$ as:

$$ A = \begin{bmatrix} 0.6 & 0.4 \\ 0.2 & 0.8 \end{bmatrix} $$

Each observed signal is a weighted combination of the two sources:

$$ \begin{cases} x_1(t) = 0.6 s_1(t) + 0.4 s_2(t), \\ x_2(t) = 0.2 s_1(t) + 0.8 s_2(t). \end{cases} $$

This simulates two microphones placed in different positions within a room, each recording a distinct mixture of two voices or signals.
